Title: Defining "Fake News" | Source: Digital Journalism, Vol. 6, No. 2, pp. 137–153 (Taylor & Francis / Routledge) | Date: 2017

The term "fake news" has become a buzzword, yet its meaning varies widely across public and academic discourse. This paper reviews 34 scholarly articles published between 2003 and 2017 to map out how the term has been defined and used, resulting in a six-type typology organized along two key dimensions: facticity (how much the content relies on real facts) and intention to deceive (whether the author aims to mislead).

The six types identified are: (1) News Satire — mock news programs like The Daily Show that use humor and exaggeration but are grounded in real events, with no intent to deceive; (2) News Parody — content like The Onion that fabricates stories for comedic effect, relying on a shared understanding between author and audience that it is not real; (3) Fabrication — false stories presented as genuine news with the deliberate intent to mislead, driven by political or financial motives; (4) Manipulation — doctored images or videos used to create false narratives; (5) Advertising/PR — promotional content (e.g., video news releases, native advertising, clickbait) disguised as legitimate journalism; and (6) Propaganda — politically motivated content designed to shape public perception, often by state or partisan actors.

A key insight is that all six types mimic the appearance and credibility of real news, which is what makes them effective and dangerous. The authors also stress that the audience plays a co-constructive role — fake news only fully becomes "fake news" when audiences mistake it for real, particularly in social media environments where popularity signals (likes, shares) substitute for verification.

The paper argues that current popular usage of "fake news" tends to focus narrowly on fabrication (low facticity, high deceptive intent), while ignoring other forms. This narrow usage has also been weaponized to discredit legitimate journalism. The typology offered aims to give researchers and the public clearer conceptual tools to study, discuss, and counter the phenomenon.
