Title: A Survey on Automated Fact-Checking | Source: Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics, Vol. 10, pp. 178–206 (MIT Press) | Date: 2022

As misinformation spreads faster than ever through digital media, manual fact-checking by journalists — a process that can take hours or days — is no longer sufficient to keep pace. This survey reviews the state of automated fact-checking research, unifying fragmented prior work into a single comprehensive NLP framework with three core stages: claim detection, evidence retrieval, and claim verification (which itself breaks into verdict prediction and justification production).

Claim detection is the first step — identifying which statements are even worth checking. This is typically framed around "check-worthiness" (claims the public would care about verifying) or "checkability" (whether objective evidence exists to assess a claim at all). Rumor detection on social media is a related application, using metadata like likes and shares alongside text to flag unverified circulating statements.

Evidence retrieval involves finding external sources — text, tables, knowledge bases, images — that can confirm or refute a claim. A core challenge here is trustworthiness: most systems rely on a single authoritative source like Wikipedia, which is a significant limitation for real-world use. Additionally, advances in AI-generated text have made this harder, as machine-written content can appear more credible than human-written text.

Verdict prediction assigns a truthfulness label to a claim based on retrieved evidence, ranging from simple true/false binary labels to finer-grained scales (e.g., "mostly true," "half true") used by organizations like PolitiFact. The more nuanced multi-class labels better reflect reality but introduce challenges around consistency and noise, especially when a single statement bundles multiple sub-claims of differing accuracy.

Justification production — generating an explanation for why a verdict was reached — is emphasized as critical but underserved. Simply calling something false is often ineffective and can even backfire, reinforcing belief in the false claim. Current approaches range from highlighting attention weights to generating textual summaries, but a key unresolved problem is faithfulness: AI-generated justifications may be convincing yet fail to accurately reflect the model's actual reasoning process.

The paper identifies several major open challenges: the difficulty of handling multiple languages and modalities (images, video, audio); dataset biases that let models game benchmarks without learning real fact-checking; the subjectivity of what counts as check-worthy; and the gap between debunking (reactive) and prebunking (proactive countermessaging before misinformation spreads), which research suggests is more effective but underdeveloped in NLP.

The overarching message is that automated fact-checking has matured significantly as a field, but remains far from solving the real-world problem — particularly because current systems are narrowly scoped, English-centric, and reliant on clean, single-source evidence that doesn't reflect the messy, multilingual, multimodal information environment where misinformation actually spreads.
