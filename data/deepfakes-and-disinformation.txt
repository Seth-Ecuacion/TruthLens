Title: Deepfakes and Disinformation: Exploring the Impact of Synthetic Political Video on Deception, Uncertainty, and Trust in News | Source: Vaccari, C. & Chadwick, A. | Date: 2020

Deepfakes do not necessarily deceive people outright, but they reliably generate uncertainty about the truthfulness of content, and that uncertainty in turn erodes trust in news on social media. Using an online experiment with a representative UK sample (N=2,005), the study tested three versions of the BuzzFeed Obama/Peele deepfake — a 4-second deceptive clip, a 26-second deceptive clip, and the full educational video with the reveal — to measure deception, uncertainty, and trust outcomes.

Only 16% of participants were outright deceived regardless of treatment, but 33-37% of those exposed to deceptive versions reported uncertainty compared to 27.5% for the educational version. Crucially, that uncertainty was found to mediate a significant reduction in trust in social media news. The primary danger of deepfakes is therefore not mass deception but the cultivation of generalized indeterminacy — a condition where audiences can no longer confidently distinguish true from false, making them susceptible to cynicism and disengagement. This aligns with documented state-sponsored propaganda strategies that deliberately flood information spaces with contradiction to paralyze public judgment.

Visual content is particularly vulnerable to this dynamic because humans process images with less cognitive effort than text, are more likely to treat audiovisual content as realistic, and are susceptible to the "truthiness effect" where familiarity breeds perceived credibility. Traditional fact-checking models are poorly equipped to counter deepfakes since they assume a real person made a real statement that simply needs contextualizing — deepfakes undercut that premise entirely. Educational exposure to how deepfakes work did reduce uncertainty and partially restore trust, though it did not eliminate outright deception.
