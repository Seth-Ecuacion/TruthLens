Title: Architects of Networked Disinformation: Behind the Scenes of Troll Farms in the Philippines | Source: Ong, J. & Cabañes, J. | Date: 2018

CHAPTER 3: THE DISINFORMATION INTERFACE
Work Hierarchies and Competitive Collegialities of Disinformation Architects

Networked disinformation in the Philippines involves diverse and loosely interconnected groups of hierarchized digital workers with varied social backgrounds and distinct work responsibilities. Unlike some global models where disinformation is authored by teenagers with purely financial motivations, the Philippine architecture is organized into professionalized hierarchies.

THE HIERARCHY OF DISINFORMATION WORKERS

1. Chief Architects (Ad and PR Strategists):
At the top level are ad and PR executives who take on the role of high-level political operators. Often occupying leadership roles in "boutique agencies," they transpose industry techniques of reputation-building and "spin" to political campaigns. Their main responsibility is to generate and inspire support through online engagement, recruiting teams of influencers and operators to create "illusions of engagement." They are often wary of automated bots, preferring the labor of savvy creative writers who can mobilize populist sentiment through popular vernaculars.

2. Anonymous Digital Influencers (Key Opinion Leaders):
At the mid-level are digital influencers, including both high-profile pundits and anonymous micro-influencers. These workers operate anonymous accounts (comedy or inspirational pages) that entertain followers while slipping in paid political content. They act as "attention-hackers," translating campaign plans into shareable content using snark, humor, or inspirational messaging. Many are freelance digital workers who view this as a lucrative "sideline" to their primary jobs in IT or corporate marketing.

3. Community-level Fake Account Operators:
At the lowest level are operators tasked with script-based work. They post written or visual content designed by strategists on a predetermined schedule. Their role is to amplify messages through likes and shares to create bandwagon effects, driving real grassroots supporters to express enthusiasm. They often use ad hominem attacks or quarrels to shut down opponents. Many are junior-level employees in political administrative staffs or freelancers paid on a per-day basis.

MORAL JUSTIFICATIONS AND THE "ETHICS OF IRONY"

Disinformation architects employ various denial strategies to displace moral responsibility. Workers across the hierarchy rarely self-identify as "trolls," instead projecting that label onto unnamed "supervillains" or "real" enthusiastic supporters.

- Top-level strategists use "gamification" and "fictionalization" to justify their work, treating the dangerous consequences of their actions as a game where the only failure is being "found out."
- Mid-level influencers use "normalization," citing that they do the same work to promote corporate brands or beauty pageants.
- Low-level operators cite financial necessity, often viewing the work as a temporary "roller coaster ride" to achieve material stability.

COMPETITIVE COLLEGIALITIES

This concept describes the complicated work relationships among disinformation producers. While workers find themselves compelled to work together, their professional aspirations are at odds. Higher-level architects want to maintain status hierarchies, while lower-level operators aspire for upward mobility. This leads to a "Wild West" environment where influencers may compete to outdo each other in crafting viral, incivil messages to secure higher remuneration based on engagement rates.

THE DISINFORMATION INTERFACE

The "disinformation interface" captures the intersection where professional, paid architects attempt to inspire "real" political supporters. It is a liminal space where the boundaries between "paid worker" and "fan" are porous. The success of a campaign often depends on the "promotional labor" of real supporters who take original messages and amplify them in everyday spaces. This interface relies on "emotional literacies"—rationalities that guide engagement based on cultural narratives and personal experiences.

CASE STUDY PORTRAITS

Portrait of a Chief Architect (Dom): A seasoned creative director who describes political consultancy as a more thrilling version of handling corporate brands. She maintains a 20% "passion" project to keep her sanity while the other 80% of her work involves "cleaning up" political messes for survival.

Portrait of an Anonymous Influencer (Georgina): A transgender digital marketer who uses specialized skills in search engine optimization (SEO) and "quote accounts" to drive traffic. She uses "gender code-switching" to deploy the right personas to broadcast deceptive messages in the most seemingly authentic way possible.

Portrait of a Reluctant Troll (Risa): A fresh college graduate tasked with creating fake accounts for a mayoral candidate without additional pay. Her discomfort was evident in her stilted narration, viewing the work as a temporary compromise to secure recommendation letters for law school.
