Title: Deepfake Detection: A Systematic Literature Review | Source: IEEE Access, Vol. 10, pp. 25494–25513 (IEEE) | Date: 2022

The term "Deepfake" — derived from "deep learning" and "fake" — refers to photo-realistic manipulated images, videos, or audio generated using AI, most notably Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). First emerging on Reddit in late 2017 and initially used for non-consensual pornography, deepfakes have rapidly evolved into a broad societal threat: spreading misinformation, enabling political manipulation, harassment, and blackmail. This paper conducts a systematic literature review (SLR) of 112 studies published between 2018 and 2020, organizing deepfake detection research into a structured taxonomy and evaluating the comparative performance of detection methods.

Detection techniques are grouped into four categories. Deep learning-based methods are by far the most dominant, representing about 77% of studies. These rely primarily on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) — such as XceptionNet, ResNet, and VGG — as well as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and ensemble models that combine multiple architectures. These methods detect manipulations by analyzing facial artifacts, spatiotemporal inconsistencies, biological signals (e.g., heartbeat rhythms, eye blinking), and frequency domain patterns. Machine learning-based methods (18% of studies) use classifiers like Support Vector Machines (SVM) and Random Forests, relying on handcrafted features such as facial landmarks and head pose inconsistencies — achieving up to 98% accuracy when trained and tested on the same dataset, but dropping sharply on unseen data. Statistical methods (3%) examine signal-level properties, such as camera sensor noise patterns (PRNU) and distribution distances between real and GAN-generated content. Blockchain-based approaches (2%) take a provenance-verification angle, using decentralized ledgers to trace a video's origin and prove its authenticity rather than analyzing content directly.

The detection pipeline across all studies follows a common six-stage process: data collection, face detection, feature extraction, feature selection, model selection, and model evaluation. The most widely used benchmark datasets are FaceForensics++, Celeb-DF, and the Deepfake Detection Challenge (DFDC). Performance is most commonly assessed using accuracy and AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve), with deep learning methods achieving a mean accuracy of 89.73% and AUC of 0.917 — outperforming non-deep learning methods on both accuracy and precision.

A key challenge the paper identifies is the lack of a standardized evaluation framework: studies use inconsistent datasets, varying dataset sizes, and different metrics, making fair cross-study comparisons difficult. The authors also note that most deep learning models tend to overfit to their training data and perform poorly in cross-dataset scenarios, meaning real-world generalizability remains a serious unsolved problem. The central takeaway is that while deep learning — particularly CNN-based ensemble methods — is the current state of the art for deepfake detection, the field is in an ongoing arms race: as generation techniques improve, detection methods must continuously adapt, and no single approach is yet robust enough for universal deployment.
